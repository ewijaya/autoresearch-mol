\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Transformer architectures developed for natural language processing have been widely adopted for molecular modeling, from property prediction \citep{chithrananda2020chemberta,ross2022molformer} to protein structure \citep{lin2023esm2} and 3D molecular representations \citep{zhou2023unimol}. In nearly all cases, the transformer architecture is borrowed from NLP with minimal modification, implicitly assuming that molecules and language share the same computational requirements. Yet molecular sequences differ from natural language in fundamental ways: SMILES strings use fewer than 50 characters, protein sequences use 20 amino acid tokens, and both are far shorter than typical NLP contexts. Whether these differences warrant distinct architectural designs has not been systematically investigated.

Neural architecture search (NAS) offers a principled way to discover domain-specific designs \citep{elsken2019nas}, but traditional NAS operates over hand-designed search spaces that may miss important structural innovations. Recent work has shown that large language models can serve as architecture search agents, proposing open-ended code modifications that go beyond predefined search spaces \citep{chen2023evoprompting,romeraparedes2024funsearch,hu2025lmsearcher}. The autoresearch framework \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch} demonstrated that an LLM agent can iteratively improve a transformer training script for NLP through autonomous code modifications. However, this line of work has been limited to natural language, and no prior work has decomposed how much of the improvement comes from architectural changes versus hyperparameter tuning.

This paper makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{A controlled decomposition of architecture search vs.\ HP tuning.} We design a 4-condition experiment (LLM-guided agent, random NAS, HP-only agent, fixed default) that cleanly separates the contributions of architecture search and hyperparameter tuning across three domains (SMILES, protein, NLP), totaling 3{,}106 experiments.

\item \textbf{Evidence that the value of architecture search is domain-dependent.} On NLP, architecture search contributes 81\% of total improvement over the baseline ($p = 0.008$); on SMILES, HP tuning alone captures 151\% of the improvement ($p = 0.001$) and architecture search is counterproductive. For proteins, margins are too small for either component to reach significance.

\item \textbf{A surprising universality finding.} While agent-discovered architectures cluster by domain ($p = 0.004$), all 41 innovations transfer across domains with $<$1\% degradation ($p = 2 \times 10^{-19}$ against the predicted 35\% universal rate). The clustering reflects search path dependence, not fundamental domain requirements at this scale.
\end{enumerate}

These findings yield a practical decision framework: for domains with small vocabularies and short sequences (like SMILES), practitioners should constrain their search to hyperparameters only; for domains with large vocabularies and long sequences (like NLP), full architecture search accounts for the majority of gains. We release the complete framework, all 3{,}106 experiment logs, and the agent prompts at \url{https://github.com/ewijaya/autoresearch-mol}.
